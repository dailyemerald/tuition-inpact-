Here at the University of Oregon, half of the 2014 undergraduate class had borrowed student loans. Those who had left with an average loan debt of $24,508.

According to Director of Financial Aid and Scholarships Jim Brooks, the University of Oregon has limited funds to disburse financial aid to students as a state institution.

In dollars, the Department of Financial Aid and Scholarships disburses about $235 million dollars a year in aid for students. About $178 million of that is aid that comes from the federal government.

High-need students can receive the Federal Pell Grant - a federal allocation disbursed to students who usually have parental incomes of less than $50,000 - in addition to loans.

About 25 percent of all undergraduate students received the Federal Pell Grant during the 2013-2014 school year. This works out to around 37 percent of resident undergraduates and eight percent of nonresident undergraduates – about 5,400 students each year.

In the 2015-2016 school year the maximum a student can receive on the Pell Grant is $5,775. And with in-state tuition estimated at $10,288.50 and out of state tuition at $34,039.50 for the same year (not including the cost of books or living expenses), many students - even the pell eligible ones - are still strapped for cash and forced into loans.

The University of Oregon hopes to raise money for financial aid through the current capital campaign in order to “put a dent in the cost for students.” Recently, Connie Balmer, a member of the UO Board of Trustees, and her husband Steve Ballmer, a former CEO of Microsoft, donated $25 million to help fund Pathway Oregon, a scholarship program that covers education costs for pell-eligible Oregon natives.

$2billion capital campaign $25 million for Pathway Oregon

As of Fall 2013, there were 1,629 students in the pathway Oregon program. That’s 12.5 percent of the in-state students enrolled that fall. For many of the other 11,500 in-state students and 11,419 out-of-state students, paying for school required a bit more.

And the cost keeps climbing.

For Brooks, working with high-need students, especially high-need nonresident students for whom the costs of attending UO are significantly higher, involves hard conversations about the realities of funding their education.

“Usually the conversation is, ‘this is my dream school, I've worked hard to get here, how do I stay here?’" Brooks said. “Those are difficult conversations.”
